How This Works

D textbook quantum mechanics

Punch in your problem, get the propagator K(xᵇ,T; xᵃ,0) = ⟨xᵇ|e−iHT/ℏ|xᵃ⟩, the amplitude to go from a to b in time T — the quantity the path integral computes. Natural units ℏ = 1. Solved by split-operator time evolution — literally the time-sliced path integral, kinetic step exact in momentum space, potential step exact in position space — run live on this device with two-level Richardson extrapolation (three source widths 2σ:σ:σ/2, cancelling σ² and σ⁴ errors) to the δ-function endpoint, and checked against Feynman's closed forms where they exist. Bound-state energies come from a separate live diagonalization. The Accuracy selector trades runtime for grid resolution and time-slice count; every result ships with a convergence report so you can see whether the extrapolation actually settled — a ⚠ warning appears when it did not.

1. Why the i keeps the wave bounded

iℏ ∂ψ/∂t = Hψ  →  ψ(t) = e−iEt/ℏψ(0)

This is Schrödinger’s equation. The i is the entire reason quantum amplitudes rotate rather than grow — it turns the exponential from a real number (which explodes or dies) into a phasor spinning on the unit circle. Norm is exactly conserved. The spinning dot on the right is e−iEt/ℏ running in real time.

D

2. From Schrödinger to Feynman — what the path integral sums

K(xᵇ,T; xᵃ,0) = ∫𝒟x(t) eiS[x]/ℏ  —  every path gets phase eiS/ℏ, paths near the classical one add coherently, others cancel

Feynman (1948) showed Schrödinger’s equation is equivalent to summing eiS/ℏ over every possible path from xᵃ to xᵇ. The i in the exponent is the same i as above — each path contributes a unit phasor, not a real weight. Paths near the classical trajectory (δS = 0) have nearly the same phase and add up. Random paths have random phases and cancel. The propagator on the Path Integral tab computes this sum numerically.

4. Euler / Catalan product — primes sort into two chirality classes

G = β(2) = ∏p odd prime (1 − χ−4(p)/p²)−1 ≈ 0.9159… (Catalan’s constant)

Every odd prime falls into one of two classes: p≡1 mod 4 (χ = +1, gold, C₁) or p≡3 mod 4 (χ = −1, blue, C₂). This is the same split as in the Gaussian integers Z[i]: p≡1 mod 4 splits as (a+bi)(a−bi) — two conjugate factors; p≡3 mod 4 stays inert — remains prime in Z[i]. The C₁/C₂ labels are exactly the split/inert distinction, not a separate GBP invention.

N = 15 is the minimum modulus that contains both a split prime (5, C₁) and an inert prime (3, C₂) as factors — both chirality classes simultaneously present in the correction. Below 15: N = 12 has only 3 (pure C₂); N = 10 has only 5 (pure C₁). N = 30 = 2×15 adds the prime 2 (the ramified prime in Z[i], the only one that neither splits nor stays inert) to get φ(30) = 8 lanes. The correction factor 15/16 at N = 30 is exact. See: Gaussian Prime Physics paper (Zenodo 10.5281/zenodo.21050302) for the full derivation.

A note on the mathematics

Sophus Lie (1842–1899) built Lie algebra to classify symmetries by zooming into the identity transformation and reading off the tangent vectors there. His machinery works perfectly once you have a smooth continuous group — and the Standard Model runs on Lie groups.

What Lie algebra cannot do is tell you which Lie group physics uses. SU(3) has 8 generators and describes 3 colors, but “why N = 3” is a question Lie theory accepts as input, not output.

Z₃₀* is a finite discrete group — no tangent space, no smooth manifold, nothing to differentiate. φ(30) = 8 is pure number theory. The claim is that the discrete structure selects the Lie group, and the continuous Lie machinery takes over from there. Ramanujan wrote down mock theta functions in 1920 with no idea they would describe black hole entropy a century later. Euler’s totient function is from the 1700s. The pieces were always there.
