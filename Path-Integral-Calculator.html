Your Problem

The virtual-particle dial D: exchanging a particle of mass μ between two charges produces V(x) = −g·e−μ|x|/√(x²+a²) — force range = 1/μ, the mediator's Compton wavelength. μ = 0 is the photon: Coulomb, infinite range. Crank μ up and watch the bound states below get shallower and die — that's why the weak force is short-ranged (W/Z are heavy) and electromagnetism is not. This is Yukawa's 1935 argument that predicted the pion.

Allowed: numbers, x, + - * / ^ ( ), sin cos tan exp log sqrt abs. Example: 0.5*x^2, 5*(abs(x)<1), exp(-x^2)

1. Feynman Path Integral

K(x,x0,t) = ∫ D[x(t)] · e^(iS[x]/ħ)
Sum the phase e^(iS/ħ) over every possible path from x0 to x, not just the classical one.
Feynman's own trick: discretize time into N slices, turning the infinite path integral into a finite multi-dimensional ordinary integral.
H
GBP's reinterpretation: instead of infinitely many time-slices, use the 8 coprime residue lanes as a finite, exactly-summable stand-in for "the set of paths." W-gate = phase per path; H2-gate = the actual interference/recombination step.

The Feynman math above is textbook and exact. Treating the 8 lanes as literally "the paths" is GBP's own framing — a structural analogy, not something derived from Feynman's construction itself.

Real, shared mathematical machinery: all five objects above involve either (a) summing a phase e2πix over a restricted or infinite index set, or (b) a cos²/sin² interference weight. That much is genuinely common ground.
What's NOT established: that these are literally the same underlying phenomenon. We checked this directly and found real counterexamples:

Quantum Search (Grover's Algorithm)

This is the real mechanism industry quantum computers use for "try every route": put all routes in superposition, mark the best one (oracle), then amplify it (diffusion), repeated ~π/4·√N times. Two honest catches:

1. Building the oracle requires already knowing the best route (computed via Held-Karp above) — on a classical simulator, that means we're demonstrating the mechanism, not gaining any speed over just using that answer directly. Real speedup needs physical quantum hardware evaluating the oracle in superposition, which this is not.

2. This uses ordinary floating-point amplitudes, NOT the exact Q(ζ30) engine — Grover's rotation angle is generally irrational, so it doesn't fit the exact cyclotomic-integer structure used elsewhere in this app.

3. With a single marked route, Grover's evolution stays exactly in the 2D plane spanned by the best route and the uniform superposition of the rest, so two scalars reproduce the full amplitude dynamics with zero approximation — memory is no longer the limit. The cap is now 12 stops, set by the time to scan (n−1)! routes when building the oracle, not by memory.

Bell State

|Ψ> = H(q0) · CNOT(q0→q1) |00> = (|00> + |11>)/√2

Why this stays on the textbook simulator: a Bell state is "two qubits entangled, third factored out." Exhaustive search over every circuit of up to 4 native GBP gates (all W(m), all A(u), H2 — 436 distinct reachable states) finds zero Bell-class states: only product states, GHZ-class states, and other fully-collective classes appear D. Structural reason (interpretive H): every native gate acts on all three encoded qubits at once — W and A act through the group structure of Z30*, and H2 pairs each lane r with −r, which flips all three bits simultaneously. Nothing in the native set is 2-local, and Bell states require a 2-local operation. Real IBM hardware context: on IBM's current Heron r2 processors, typical two-qubit gate error rates are below 10⁵³ per gate; this app's simulation itself is exact/float, not run on real hardware.

GHZ State (3 qubits)

|Ψ> = CNOT(q0→q2)·CNOT(q0→q1)·H(q0) |000> = (|000> + |111>)/√2

Standard three-qubit generalization of the Bell state — genuinely maximal entanglement across all three qubits, textbook construction.

GHZ two ways: textbook vs GBP engine D

GBP route: one unitarized H2 gate. Under the lane→3-bit encoding, H2 pairs lane index 0 (bits 000) with index 7 (bits 111) — the r ↔ −r pairing of Z30* IS the GHZ generator.

Quantum Fourier Transform (3 qubits)

QFT|j> = (1/√8) Σk=0..7 e2πijk/8|k>

Standard textbook QFT circuit (H + controlled phase rotations + swap). The 3-qubit QFT is the Fourier transform over the cyclic group Z8. GBP's native phase machinery (the W gates) supplies the characters of Z30* ≅ Z2×Z4 instead — a different group, so the two transforms cannot coincide, and this preset stays on the textbook simulator by necessity, not omission. The natural "GBP Fourier" would be the Z2×Z4 transform, which the lane structure supports natively.

Glossary

Plain-language definitions of terms used throughout this app. D = standard, textbook definition. H = GBP-specific term or usage.

D

Superposition

A quantum state that is a weighted combination of two or more distinct possibilities at once, rather than being in just one of them. Not "unknown which one" — genuinely both, until measured.
D

Amplitude

A complex number attached to each possible outcome in a superposition. Its squared magnitude (|amplitude|²) gives the probability of seeing that outcome when you measure. Amplitudes can be positive, negative, or have a phase — probabilities can't.
D

Phase

The "angle" part of a complex amplitude (think of it as a clock hand's direction, separate from its length). Two amplitudes with the same magnitude but different phase look identical in probability but interfere differently when combined.
D

Interference

When two amplitudes for the same outcome are added together, their phases determine whether they reinforce (constructive) or cancel (destructive) — the same math as overlapping waves. This is the mechanism quantum algorithms use to boost right answers and suppress wrong ones.
D

Measurement

The act of reading out a definite classical outcome from a superposition. Afterward, the superposition is gone — you get one result, with probability given by that outcome's |amplitude|². This app's simulators track amplitudes only; none of the tabs perform an actual probabilistic measurement/collapse step.
D

Entanglement

A correlation between two or more qubits/lanes stronger than anything possible classically: measuring one instantly determines what you'll get from the other, no matter the distance between them. Bell and GHZ states (Presets tab) are the standard textbook examples.
D

Qubit

The basic 2-level unit of standard quantum computing (a "quantum bit"): a superposition of |0> and |1>. Used only in the Presets tab's textbook simulator — the GBP engine uses 8-lane sectors instead, see "Lane" below.
H

Lane (GBP-specific)

One of the 8 coprime residues mod 30 — {1, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, 23, 29} — that GBP uses as its basic unit instead of a 2-level qubit. A "sector" is one 8-lane block; stacking n sectors gives a dim-8ⁿ state space, tracked as "3n qubit-equivalent" for rough size comparison only, not a claim of literal qubit equivalence.
D

Cyclotomic integer / Q(ζ30)

A number built from integer combinations of powers of a primitive 30th root of unity ζ30 = e2πi/30. Storing amplitudes this way (as the Cyc class in this app, using BigInt coefficients) keeps every calculation exact — no floating-point rounding, ever, no matter how many gates are chained.
D

BigInt

JavaScript's arbitrary-precision integer type. Ordinary numbers lose precision past ~2&sup5;³; BigInt doesn't, which is what lets the exact engine grow without drift. (This app also had to guard against a real bug: converting a huge BigInt to a float for on-screen display can silently overflow before any division happens — fixed by dividing in BigInt space first.)
D

Bell state

The simplest maximally-entangled 2-qubit state, (|00>+|11>)/√2, built from one Hadamard (H) gate and one CNOT gate. Standard textbook construction — see the Presets tab.
D

GHZ state

The 3+ qubit generalization of a Bell state, (|00...0>+|11...1>)/√2, where all qubits collapse together rather than just pairs.
D

QFT (Quantum Fourier Transform)

The quantum analogue of a discrete Fourier transform: takes a single "spike" basis state and spreads it into a uniform-magnitude superposition where all the original information sits in the relative phases instead. Core subroutine of Shor's algorithm and phase estimation.
H

W / A / H2 gates (GBP-specific)

W(m): multiplies each lane's amplitude by a phase depending on m and that lane's residue — a phase/rotation gate. A(u): permutes the 8 lanes by multiplication mod 30 — a permutation gate. H2: pairwise add/subtract across 4 fixed lane-pairs — the interference/mixing step, deliberately unnormalized (magnitude roughly doubles each triple) so the Step Through tab can show real float drift for comparison.
D

MAPE / RMSE

Mean Absolute Percentage Error and Root Mean Square Error — standard statistical measures of how far predicted values (here, GBP's predicted baryon masses) are from measured ones. Lower is better; both are computed the same way regardless of what framework produced the predictions.
