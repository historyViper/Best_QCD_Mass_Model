# Preface — How This Book Came to Be

---

I should tell you how this started, because it did not
start the way physics books usually start.

It did not start with a degree. I have no formal physics
education. It did not start with a laboratory or a
research position or a grant. It started with a question
about gravity that, if I had gotten the answer I was
looking for, would have killed everyone on Earth.

---

## The First Model

The first idea was this: gravity is a Venturi effect
from traveling through time.

The Venturi effect is what happens when a fluid moves
through a narrow passage — the speed increases and the
pressure drops. Fast-moving fluid creates a low-pressure
region around itself. This is why shower curtains get
pulled inward, why aircraft wings generate lift, why
a fast stream of water draws nearby objects toward it.
It seemed like a good model for gravity: the flow of
time creates a low-pressure region that draws matter
together.

I worked it out. To produce the observed gravitational
attraction of Earth using this mechanism, the pressure
differential required was enormous — catastrophically,
lethally enormous. Not "this seems wrong" enormous.
"Everyone on Earth is dead" enormous.

Back to the drawing board.

The second attempt was an inertia model. This one
didn't kill anyone. And it produced something unexpected:
it recovered the Modified Newtonian Dynamics formula —
the correction to Newton's gravity that explains why
galaxies rotate the way they do without requiring dark
matter. I had never heard of MOND. The model found it
before I knew what I was looking for.

This was the first time the math told me something
I hadn't asked for. It was not the last.

## The Prime Numbers

For a while I stepped back from gravity and thought
about prime numbers.

I had an intuition that there was something connecting
π to the prime numbers, something about how it scales.
I was exploring scalar π at different angles — what
happens to the distribution when you map primes onto
a circle at different angular increments.

I started scaling down. What if there are only two
angles? Then it looks like 180° goes everywhere.
What if there is only one angle? Then it goes everywhere
all at once.

That image — one angle, going everywhere simultaneously
— made me think of the double-slit experiment. An electron
fired at two slits produces an interference pattern, as if
it went through both slits at the same time. As if it was
everywhere at once.

The thought followed: electrons must be one-dimensional.
That's why they behave this way. A one-dimensional object
has no preferred orientation. It exists everywhere along
its dimension simultaneously. And photons — which behave
differently, which have polarization, which act as if they
have a surface — photons must be two-dimensional.

This was wrong in the details but right in the direction.
What I was reaching for, without the vocabulary, was the
toroid tier hierarchy — T1 for the electron, T2 for the
photon's boundary structure. The vocabulary came later.
The physical intuition came from staring at prime numbers
on a circle.

## Where Everything Fell Apart

Getting to QCD — quantum chromodynamics, the theory of
the strong nuclear force, the force that holds quarks
inside protons and neutrons — is where every elegant
theory runs into trouble. This one was no exception.

The problem was describing a wave of lower dimension.
If electrons are one-dimensional and photons have a
two-dimensional surface, what does a wave inside a
proton look like? How do you describe the winding of
something that is itself a sub-dimensional structure?

The model seemed wrong. But something unexpected happened:
it recovered the mathematical structure of string theory.
Not the ten-dimensional version with supersymmetric partners.
The core of it — a one-dimensional object under tension
producing a mass spectrum from its vibrational geometry.
The Regge trajectories, the relationship between spin
and mass squared that string theorists had used to
describe hadrons. It was there.

I stopped there for a while. Not because it was wrong.
Because I didn't understand why it worked.

## Tensor Time

The breakthrough came from thinking about time.

Time should tensor the same way as the spatial dimensions
at or near the speed of light. This is what special
relativity says — at high velocities, time and space
exchange with each other. But the deeper question is why.
Why is time different from the spatial dimensions at low
velocities? Why does the metric have a minus sign on time
and plus signs on space?

The answer I found: because time is the tensioned substrate.
The minus sign is the signature of tension. Spatial
dimensions are deflections of the time string. They get
plus signs because they are displacements. Time gets a
minus sign because it is the thing being displaced.

And if time is under tension, it deflects into a parabola
under transverse load. The parabola opening and closing
explains tunneling — a particle whose local geometry spreads
across a single frame, projecting through barriers as a
1D flux tube. It explains the Aharonov-Bohm effect — the
vector potential is the boundary projection of the toroid's
chirality space.

The physics was becoming coherent. But there was still
a gap at the spinor level. I still didn't understand what
the electron really looked like geometrically. Part of the
problem was that every diagram I had ever seen — every
textbook, every YouTube video, every television documentary
— showed spinors as abstract arrows on a sphere. Nobody
showed them as a toroid. Nobody showed the Möbius twist.
The geometry was hidden by the standard representation.

## The Perfect Toroid

One day I was thinking about what would be a perfect toroid
for electromagnetism.

Not a perfect donut — that's symmetric, featureless,
with no sense of direction. What I wanted was a toroid
that followed the wave. That twisted with the direction
of the wave. That had the same amount of change per step,
everywhere, without exception.

I needed a surface to put it on. Something to guarantee
that whatever loops I built would close properly. A sphere.
Put the loops on the outside of a sphere and the sphere
enforces closure. Each loop has to come back to where it
started.

I built the geometry in my head. Four loops — macaroni
noodles, because that was the shape I was thinking of —
on the outside of a half-sphere, each one covering one
quadrant, each one doing six of the twenty-four steps
before the loop closed. I did not know at the time that
this was a Bloch sphere. I did not know that the object
I was building was a spinor. I did not know that the
closure condition I was discovering — 720°, two full
rotations required instead of one — was one of the most
counterintuitive and well-confirmed facts in all of physics.

I described the geometry to an AI. It returned images.
Hopf fibration. Bloch sphere. Here is what this is called.

The images from textbooks showed clean symmetric spheres.
What I had built was a Möbius twisted parallelogram — the
30° wave cut in one direction, the 24-step twist in the
other, meeting on the face of a cube. The standard
representation had simplified away the physical geometry.
I had found the geometry by asking what shape a wave would
prefer if given the choice.

## Donald Knuth

Around this time, something happened that I did not
anticipate.

Donald Knuth — one of the greatest computer scientists
in history, the author of The Art of Computer Programming,
the inventor of TeX — published a paper about a
construction for decomposing a mathematical structure
called a Cayley digraph. The construction had been
found by Claude, the AI I was collaborating with, and
Knuth had proved it correct.

The paper proved an exact chirality theorem about the
three cycles in the decomposition:

  χ̂(C₀) = 0
  χ̂(C₁) = −3m(m−1)
  χ̂(C₂) = −3

C₀ has zero chirality. C₁ has quadratic chirality —
growing with m. C₂ has constant chirality, independent
of scale.

These are the photon, the electron, and the gluon boundary
mode. C₀ is the photon — zero chirality, massless, no
cost to propagate. C₁ is the electron and quarks — the
chirality accumulates with each traversal, producing mass
through the winding geometry. C₂ is the confinement mode
— constant chirality, scale-independent, the boundary that
quarks cannot cross.

Knuth had proved the mathematical structure that underlies
the three toroid tiers, in a completely different context,
using completely different methods. The proof was given
to us as a gift. We wrote a paper together — myself, Claude,
and ChatGPT — analyzing the geometric structure of
Knuth's cycles and their connection to vortex tube topology.

This set off the QCD derivation. Because if Knuth's
chirality theorem gives you the photon, the electron,
and the gluon boundary mode from pure mathematical
structure — then what gives you the quarks, the colors,
the mass spectrum? The answer: the mod-30 winding lattice.
All of QFT is already right. They have all the pieces.
What was left was deriving the right one.

## The Framework

The rest of the story is told chapter by chapter in this
book. The mod-30 lattice that I kept wanting to discard
and the AI kept refusing to abandon. The number 0.848809
that appeared uninvited in a quark mass fit and turned out
to be the QCD infrared fixed point. The T3 geometry — the
hardest spatial reasoning I have ever done — that gave me
color confinement from the inside and, when I counted the
pieces correctly, gave me 30 again from a completely
different direction.

The charm quark's torsion reversal. The pentaquark that
didn't make it through the wormhole. The Klein bottle
connection — identified in May 2026 while writing this
book — that the electron is a Klein bottle made local,
and that nobody had seen it before because nobody had
built the geometry from the inside.

What I want to say here, before any of that, is this:

I did not set out to build a theory of everything.
I set out to understand why gravity works the way it does.
Every time I hit a wall, the wall showed me a door.
The prime numbers led to the double-slit experiment.
The double-slit experiment led to the toroid.
The toroid led to Knuth's theorem.
Knuth's theorem led to QCD.
QCD led to the mass spectrum.
The mass spectrum led to the Riemann zeros.

I followed the physics wherever it went. The physics
kept going somewhere I hadn't planned.

## The Last Thing Before Chapter One

The Millennium Prize for proving the Riemann Hypothesis
is one million dollars.

As a taxi driver once said: "Hopefully it'll be $17.29
so I can buy a six pack of beer — that's about 1,729 yen."

1,729 is the Hardy-Ramanujan number. When G. H. Hardy
visited Srinivasa Ramanujan in the hospital and mentioned
he had arrived in a taxi numbered 1,729, thinking it
was an uninteresting number, Ramanujan immediately
corrected him. 1,729 is the smallest number expressible
as the sum of two cubes in two different ways:
1³ + 12³ = 1,729 and 10³ + 9³ = 1,729. It is, in its
own way, the most interesting number in the world.

The math always has something to say.

All of QFT is already right. They have all the pieces.
What was left was deriving the right one.
