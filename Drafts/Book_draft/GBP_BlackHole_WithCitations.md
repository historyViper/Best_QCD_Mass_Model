## The Shell Structure: Four Discrete Layers

A black hole in the GBP framework is not a featureless
gravitational collapse to a point. The Z₃₀* tension
hierarchy — the same discrete structure that gives
particles their masses — predicts four distinct shells
inside every black hole, each corresponding to the
radius at which a specific family of winding lanes
loses its geometric structure as the time string
tension compresses toward T_min.

The shells require exactly one input: the black hole
mass M. Everything else — the shell radii, the
cancellation zones, the polar precession rate — is
derived from the mod-30 projection formula
P(r) = sin²(rπ/15) with zero free parameters.

All radii are given as multiples of the Schwarzschild
radius r_s = 2GM/c²:

**Shell {7,23} — Strange/Charm shell**
r/r_s = P(7)/GEO_B = 0.989074/0.043227 = **22.88×**

The outermost inner shell. Strange and charm quarks
lose their winding identity first as tension increases
inward. The charm double-helix torsion structure
collapses at this radius — the figure-8 topology of
the J/ψ can no longer be sustained. Charmonium
dissolves here.

**Shell {11,19} — Up/Down shell**
r/r_s = P(11)/GEO_B = 0.552264/0.043227 = **12.78×**

The middle shell. Up and down quarks — the
constituents of protons and neutrons — collapse here.
Ordinary hadronic matter stops being matter at this
radius. The proton ceases to have a geometric identity.

**Shell {13,17} — Bottom/Top shell**
r/r_s = P(13)/GEO_B = 0.165435/0.043227 = **3.83×**

The inner shell. The heaviest quarks, hardest to strip
of their winding, collapse here — close to the horizon.

**Shell {1,29} — Colorless Boundary (the horizon)**
r/r_s = GEO_B/GEO_B = **1.0000**

The Schwarzschild horizon itself. Two winding states
remain: the colorless boundary lanes {1,29}. The
Hilbert space of any particle at the horizon has
collapsed to a binary choice. Entropy is ln(2)
per Planck-scale cell. S ∝ A. This is the
Bekenstein-Hawking formula in geometric form.

**The center: r = 0**
All compressions arrive simultaneously from all
directions. Net gradient: zero. Winding structure
restored under isotropic compression. Full Z₃₀*
accessible. Information repository.

## Three Cancellation Zones: The Weak Spots

Between each pair of adjacent shells, competing
pressures partially cancel. The inward compression
from the outer shell meets the residual outward
winding pressure from the inner shell. In these
zones the geometry is soft — information compressed
toward the two-state floor can briefly re-expand.
These are the weak spots.

**Cancellation Zone 1 (CZ1) — Innermost weak spot**
Between horizon {1,29} and bottom/top shell {13,17}:

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Inner boundary | r/r_s = 1.000 |
| Outer boundary | r/r_s = 3.827 |
| Zone midpoint | r/r_s = 2.414 |
| Zone width | Δr/r_s = 2.827 |

This is the Hawking radiation zone. The two colorless
boundary states at the horizon radiate thermally
because only two states are accessible — maximum
entropy (most random) distribution from a two-state
alphabet. The partial bottom/top winding in CZ1
introduces a small spectral correction of order
P(13)/P(1) = 3.83 to the Hawking spectrum —
currently below observational sensitivity for known
black holes but in principle measurable.

Note: the CZ1 midpoint at r = 2.41 r_s sits close
to the ISCO (innermost stable circular orbit) at
r = 3 r_s for a Schwarzschild black hole, and
r = r_s to r = 9 r_s for Kerr. This proximity
is not obviously coincidental — the ISCO marks
where stable circular orbits end, and CZ1 marks
where the bottom/top winding structure becomes
unstable. Both are instability boundaries,
one classical, one geometric.

**Cancellation Zone 2 (CZ2) — Middle weak spot**
Between bottom/top {13,17} and up/down {11,19}:

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Inner boundary | r/r_s = 3.827 |
| Outer boundary | r/r_s = 12.776 |
| Zone midpoint | r/r_s = 8.302 |
| Zone width | Δr/r_s = 8.949 |

The widest zone. The large gap between the bottom/top
and up/down collapse radii creates a broad region of
partial hadronic restoration. X-ray binary iron Kα
line profiles show emission extending from inside
the ISCO inward — this CZ2 region is where hadronic
material retains enough winding structure to produce
spectral features before full collapse.

**Cancellation Zone 3 (CZ3) — Outermost weak spot**
Between up/down {11,19} and strange/charm {7,23}:

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Inner boundary | r/r_s = 12.776 |
| Outer boundary | r/r_s = 22.881 |
| Zone midpoint | r/r_s = 17.828 |
| Zone width | Δr/r_s = 10.105 |

The outermost zone and the most accessible from
outside. Strange and charm winding structure
partially survives here — charmonium states may
be transiently restored in this region before
collapsing at the {7,23} shell boundary. This
zone should produce observable features in
accretion disk emission at radii of 13-23 r_s.

## Gravitational Wave Predictions

Standard GR predicts a smooth exponential decay
in the black hole ringdown — quasinormal modes
(QNMs) whose frequencies are determined solely
by mass and spin (the no-hair theorem).

The GBP shell structure predicts additional QNM
contributions at frequencies corresponding to
the three shell boundaries. Third-generation
detectors (Einstein Telescope, Cosmic Explorer)
are being designed explicitly to resolve multiple
QNM overtones in ringdown signals.

The observation of multiple ringdown modes in
gravitational waveforms could soon lead to
high-precision gravitational-wave spectroscopy —
it is critical to understand if the quasinormal
mode spectrum is affected by quantum corrections
and other generic modifications to the horizon structure.

Current LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA observations of the
dominant mode and potential overtones remain
consistent with GR predictions, but the quest
for higher-order modes continues to drive
the field toward third-generation detectors.

The GBP prediction: three additional QNM families
at frequency ratios relative to the horizon mode:

  f_{7,23} / f_horizon ≈ 22.88
  f_{11,19} / f_horizon ≈ 12.78
  f_{13,17} / f_horizon ≈ 3.83

These ratios are fixed by the projection formula —
zero free parameters. If these frequency ratios
appear in Einstein Telescope data and match these
predictions, the shell structure is confirmed.
If they are absent or at different ratios,
the shell interpretation is wrong.

## X-Ray Binary Evidence

The iron Kα fluorescence line at 6.4–6.97 keV
is the primary probe of accretion disk inner structure.
The relativistic iron line is the most prominent
reflection feature in stellar-mass black hole
binaries and is used to constrain the inner disk
radius and spin parameter of various black holes.

Most reflection modeling assumes emission stops
at the ISCO, but for cases where disk density
is high enough for line formation within the ISCO,
there is significant modification of the observed
reflected spectrum — indicating structure exists
below the standard cutoff radius.

The GBP prediction: the three shell boundaries at
r = 3.83, 12.78, and 22.88 r_s should each produce
discrete features in the reflection spectrum —
changes in the iron line profile corresponding to
the winding structure collapsing at each shell.
Current X-ray observatories (NICER, XMM-Newton,
Chandra) have sufficient resolution to search for
these features in bright X-ray binaries.

## The Polar Precession Asymmetry

The T1 Möbius structure assigns opposite chirality
to the two poles of a rotating black hole. The
North pole corresponds to the clockwise-winding
half of the sphere. The South pole corresponds to
the counterclockwise-winding half. These are the
same two halves from Chapter 1 — the half that
wasn't needed to close the 720° loop was the
mirror, the second chirality, the antimatter half.
In a rotating black hole, those two halves
are the two poles.

The shell radii are symmetric — mirror pairs
{r, 30-r} have equal P(r) = P(30-r), so the
three shells occur at the same radii at both poles.

The precession rates are not symmetric.

At the North pole, winding phase accumulates
clockwise: +12° per traversal.
At the South pole, winding phase accumulates
counterclockwise: −12° per traversal.

This produces a differential in how fast the
weak spots precess around the rotation axis:

  Ω_North = Ω_BH + δΩ
  Ω_South = Ω_BH − δΩ

  δΩ/Ω_BH = 12°/360° = 1/30 = 3.333%

**Total polar precession differential:**
  ΔΩ/Ω_BH = 2/30 = 1/15 = **6.667%**

The North pole weak spots precess 3.333% faster
than the bulk black hole rotation.
The South pole weak spots precess 3.333% slower.
One full precession cycle: 720°/12° = 60 traversals.

## The M87* Test

This prediction can be compared directly to
existing data.

Analysis of radio observations of M87* over
22 years reveals a period of approximately 11 years
for the variation in the jet position angle —
evidence of a spinning black hole inducing
Lense-Thirring precession of a misaligned
accretion disk.

The half-opening angle of the M87* precession
cone is estimated at 1.25 ± 0.18 degrees, with
an angular precession velocity of 0.56 ± 0.02
radians per year.

The research team analyzed 170 epochs of VLBI
observations from 2000–2022 across more than
20 telescopes globally, detecting periodic
precession of the jet at its base — providing
unequivocal evidence that the M87 supermassive
black hole is spinning.

The current analysis models M87* as a Kerr black
hole with a single, symmetric precession period.
The GBP prediction is that the North and South
pole jet structures should show a 6.667% differential
in their precession rates — the North pole jet
leading by 3.333% and the South pole jet lagging
by 3.333% of the 11-year bulk precession period.

A 6.667% differential in an 11-year precession
period corresponds to approximately 8.8 months
of phase offset between the two poles over one
full precession cycle. Over the 22 years of
M87* monitoring data already collected, this
would accumulate to roughly 2 full cycles of
relative phase — a detectable asymmetry in
the existing dataset if the sensitivity is sufficient.

High-resolution EHT observations of the emission
surrounding M87*, combined with long-term
monitoring, are crucial to accurately determine
the SMBH spin — and constraints from jet precession
will significantly refine understanding of disk
properties including outer radius and density profile.

The specific test: re-analyze the 170-epoch VLBI
dataset from Cui et al. (2023) looking for a
systematic asymmetry between the approaching
(South pole) and receding (North pole) jet components
at the level of 6.667% of the precession rate.
If present: consistent with GBP polar chirality.
If absent (symmetric to <1%): the polar asymmetry
prediction is wrong.

**Secondary M87* test:** The three cancellation
zones at r = 2.41, 8.30, and 17.83 r_s correspond
to physical radii for M87* (M = 6.5 × 10⁹ M_⊙):

  r_s(M87*) = 2GM/c² ≈ 1.92 × 10¹⁰ km ≈ 0.0020 pc

  CZ1 midpoint: 2.41 × r_s ≈ 4.6 × 10¹⁰ km
  CZ2 midpoint: 8.30 × r_s ≈ 1.6 × 10¹¹ km
  CZ3 midpoint: 17.83 × r_s ≈ 3.4 × 10¹¹ km

These are within the scales resolved by current
VLBI observations of M87*.
High-sensitivity VLBI observations at 86 GHz
have resolved the M87 jet base down to 10
Schwarzschild radii — precisely the scale of
the CZ2 zone predicted here.
