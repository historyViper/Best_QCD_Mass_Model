## The Shell Structure

A black hole is not a featureless collapse to a point.

The Z₃₀* tension hierarchy — the same discrete structure
that gives particles their masses — creates a sequence of
four distinct shells inside every black hole. Each shell
is the radius at which a specific family of winding lanes
loses its geometric structure as the time string tension
compresses toward T_min.

The shells are not free parameters. They are fixed by the
projection formula P(r) = sin²(rπ/15) applied to the
four mirror pairs of the mod-30 lattice. All radii are
given as fractions of the Schwarzschild radius r_s = 2GM/c²:

**Shell {7,23} — Strange/Charm shell**
r/r_s = P(7)/GEO_B = 0.989074 / 0.043227 = **22.88×**
The outermost inner shell. Strange and charm quarks lose
their winding identity first as tension increases inward.
This is the shell where charm's double-helix torsion
structure first collapses.

**Shell {11,19} — Up/Down shell**
r/r_s = P(11)/GEO_B = 0.552264 / 0.043227 = **12.78×**
The middle shell. Up and down quarks — the constituents
of protons and neutrons — collapse here. Matter as we
know it stops being matter at this radius.

**Shell {13,17} — Bottom/Top shell**
r/r_s = P(13)/GEO_B = 0.165435 / 0.043227 = **3.83×**
The inner shell. Bottom and top quarks — the heaviest
fermions, the ones hardest to strip of their winding —
collapse here, close to the horizon.

**Shell {1,29} — Colorless Boundary (the horizon)**
r/r_s = 1.0000
The Schwarzschild horizon itself. The two colorless
boundary lanes — GEO_B = sin²(π/15) — are the last
surviving winding states. Everything else is gone.
Two states remain. The alphabet of the black hole
has been reduced to a binary choice.

**The center: r = 0**
All compressions arrive simultaneously. Net gradient:
zero. The Hilbert space restores. Full Z₃₀* accessible.
Information repository. (See previous section.)

## Three Cancellation Zones — The Weak Spots

Between each pair of adjacent shells, there is a zone
where the winding pressure from the inner shell pushing
outward meets the compression from the outer shell
pushing inward. These opposing pressures partially cancel.

In a cancellation zone, the local geometry is not at
equilibrium — it is between two collapse thresholds,
with neither structure dominant. This creates a zone
of partial winding restoration: a place where information
that has been compressed toward the two-state floor
can briefly re-expand, where the geometry is soft enough
to carry a signal outward.

These are the weak spots.

**Cancellation Zone 1 (CZ1) — Innermost weak spot**
Between horizon and bottom/top shell:
- Inner boundary: r/r_s = 1.000 (horizon)
- Outer boundary: r/r_s = 3.827 (bottom/top shell)
- Zone midpoint:  r/r_s = 2.414
- Zone width:     Δr/r_s = 2.827

This is the region where Hawking radiation originates
in the standard picture. In GBP: the two colorless
boundary lanes {1,29} at the horizon are in tension
with the bottom/top shell trying to reassert itself
just outside. The partial cancellation here allows
the boundary states to radiate — thermal emission
from the two surviving states. This IS Hawking
radiation. It is thermal because only two states
are accessible at the horizon, and two states give
a maximum entropy (most random) distribution.

**Cancellation Zone 2 (CZ2) — Middle weak spot**
Between bottom/top and up/down shells:
- Inner boundary: r/r_s = 3.827
- Outer boundary: r/r_s = 12.776
- Zone midpoint:  r/r_s = 8.302
- Zone width:     Δr/r_s = 8.949

The widest zone. The large gap between the bottom/top
collapse radius and the up/down collapse radius creates
a broad region of partial restoration. This is where
hadronic-scale information — encoded in the up/down
winding structure — has the most opportunity to leak
outward before being re-compressed.

**Cancellation Zone 3 (CZ3) — Outermost weak spot**
Between up/down and strange/charm shells:
- Inner boundary: r/r_s = 12.776
- Outer boundary: r/r_s = 22.881
- Zone midpoint:  r/r_s = 17.828
- Zone width:     Δr/r_s = 10.105

The outermost zone. Strange and charm winding structure
partially survives here, just inside the radius where
it first begins to collapse. This is the most accessible
weak spot from outside — the closest to the surface
where external probes (gravitational waves, accreting
matter) might detect the discrete shell structure.

## What the Weak Spots Predict

**Gravitational wave signatures.**
A merging binary black hole produces gravitational waves
whose ringdown encodes the black hole's internal structure.
The standard prediction is a smooth exponential decay —
a single quasinormal mode dominated by the horizon geometry.
The GBP shell structure predicts additional quasinormal
modes corresponding to the three shell boundaries,
at frequency ratios:

  f_CZ3 / f_horizon ≈ 22.88
  f_CZ2 / f_horizon ≈ 12.78
  f_CZ1 / f_horizon ≈ 3.83

These are the ratios P(r)/GEO_B for each mirror pair —
the same numbers as the shell radii. Current gravitational
wave detectors (LIGO, Virgo) may not have the sensitivity
to resolve these higher-frequency modes in current data,
but next-generation detectors (Einstein Telescope, Cosmic
Explorer) should.

**Accretion disk structure.**
Material falling into a black hole loses its internal
geometric structure at each shell boundary. At each
shell radius, there should be a discrete change in
the emission spectrum as that family of quarks loses
its winding identity and deposits its binding energy.
Three shells → three spectral features at the predicted
radius ratios 22.88 : 12.78 : 3.83 relative to the
Schwarzschild radius.

**Hawking radiation temperature correction.**
Standard Hawking radiation assumes a featureless horizon.
The CZ1 zone — the partial restoration between the horizon
and the bottom/top shell — modifies the radiation spectrum
slightly. The two colorless boundary states at the horizon
radiate thermally, but the partial bottom/top winding in
CZ1 introduces a correction term of order P(13)/P(1) = 3.83
to the spectrum. This is a small correction but in principle
measurable for black holes close enough to emit detectable
Hawking radiation (currently theoretical — no black hole
small enough has been observed to Hawking radiate).

## The Polar Asymmetry

A rotating black hole — described in standard GR by the
Kerr metric — has an axis of rotation. The North and South
poles of the rotation axis are treated symmetrically in
the standard picture. The geometry is the same at both poles.

In GBP it is not.

The T1 Möbius structure — the same half-sphere geometry
from Chapter 1 — assigns opposite chirality to the two
poles. The North pole corresponds to the clockwise-winding
half of the sphere. The South pole corresponds to the
counterclockwise-winding half. These are the two halves
that didn't need each other to close the 720° loop —
each half is complete on its own, but they wind in
opposite directions.

This means the three weak spots precess differently
at the two poles.

**At the North pole:** The winding accumulates phase
in the clockwise direction. Each traversal of the
winding cycle adds +12° of phase (the same 12° residual
that appears in the charm quark derivation — not
coincidentally, since charm is the lane closest to
the Möbius flip point). The weak spots at the North
pole lead the bulk rotation of the black hole.

**At the South pole:** The winding accumulates phase
in the counterclockwise direction. Each traversal
subtracts 12° of phase. The weak spots at the South
pole lag the bulk rotation.

The positions of the weak spots — which shell radii
they occur at — are symmetric between poles. The
mirror pairs {r, 30-r} have equal projection weights:
P(r) = P(30-r) always. So the shell at r=12.78 r_s
exists at both poles at the same radius.

But the precession rate of each weak spot around the
rotation axis differs between poles:

  Ω_North = Ω_BH + δΩ
  Ω_South = Ω_BH − δΩ

where δΩ/Ω_BH = 12°/360° = 1/30 = 0.03333...

**The predicted polar precession differential:**

  ΔΩ/Ω_BH = (Ω_North − Ω_South) / Ω_BH
           = 2 × (1/30)
           = 1/15
           = 6.667%

The North pole weak spots precess 3.333% faster than
the bulk black hole rotation.
The South pole weak spots precess 3.333% slower.
The total differential between poles: 6.667% of the
bulk rotation rate.

This is not a small effect. 6.667% differential in
precession rate between the poles of a rotating black
hole is potentially observable in:

- Jet precession: black holes with relativistic jets
  launched along the rotation axis. If the North and
  South jets draw from weak spots at different
  precession phases, the two jets should precess at
  slightly different rates. The differential should
  be approximately 1/15 of the jet rotation period.

- Gravitational wave asymmetry: the ringdown of a
  rotating black hole should show a slight asymmetry
  between the + and × polarization modes at
  frequencies corresponding to the shell structure,
  because the modes coupling to the North pole
  and the modes coupling to the South pole have
  different phase accumulation rates.

- X-ray binary timing: in X-ray binaries where
  the black hole's spin is inferred from the
  iron Kα line, the line should show a slight
  asymmetry between redshifted (approaching South
  pole) and blueshifted (approaching North pole)
  components at the level of 1/30 of the bulk
  rotation velocity.

**Precession cycle:**
One full precession cycle requires the weak spot to
complete 720°/12° = 60 traversals of the winding
structure. The characteristic frequency of the polar
asymmetry signal is therefore 1/60 of the fundamental
winding frequency of the black hole.

## Free Parameter Note

The shell radii are given as ratios r/r_s. The absolute
radii require knowing the black hole mass M:

  r_shell = (P(r)/GEO_B) × r_s = (P(r)/GEO_B) × 2GM/c²

The only input is the black hole mass M — a single
observable measurement, not a free parameter of the
framework. Everything else follows from the Z₃₀*
projection weights, which are derived from the
parabolic winding geometry with zero freedom.

The prediction structure is: give us the mass, we
give you the shell radii, the weak spot positions,
the precession differential, and the Hawking
correction term. All from one input, one formula,
no fitting.
