## 1. The Standard View and What's Wrong With It

Standard physics treats dimensions as a container — a pre-existing stage on which objects perform. Space exists whether or not anything is in it. Time flows whether or not anyone clocks it. The extra dimensions of string theory are curled up somewhere, waiting to be found. Probability is a fundamental feature of nature: things genuinely are in superposition until observed, and observation genuinely collapses something real.

This picture has three deep problems that are usually accepted as mysteries:

**Problem 1 — The measurement problem.** Why does observation collapse a wavefunction? What counts as an observer? Why does the Born rule give the probabilities it gives? Standard QM provides no mechanism — it postulates the collapse and moves on.

**Problem 2 — The extra dimensions problem.** String theory needs 10 or 11 dimensions. We observe 3+1. The standard answer is that the remaining 6 or 7 are compactified — curled up at the Planck scale, too small to detect. But why those dimensions? Why compactified? Why not observable?

**Problem 3 — The time problem.** Time in standard physics is either a dimension like space (relativity) or an external parameter (quantum mechanics). Neither picture explains why time flows in one direction, why clocks in different gravitational fields run at different rates in a way that feels personal rather than global, or why time dilation is always between objects and never between an object and empty spacetime.

GBP resolves all three by making one move: **dimensions are properties of objects, not properties of the universe.**

## 4. Probability Is Projection

The second key sentence: *"Probability isn't randomness. It's projection."*

### The Programming Analogy

In computer graphics you render a 3D world onto a 2D screen. This is a projection — three dimensions mapped onto two. The projection is determined by the camera angle.

Consider a line in 3D space. From one camera angle it looks long. Rotate the camera 90° and the same line looks like a point. The line did not change. The projection changed. If you only had access to the 2D screen and not the 3D world, you would say: "The probability of finding length here is high; the probability of finding length there is low."
