## The Causality Problem

When the parabolic deflection of the time string first appeared as
a candidate for the field geometry, there was an immediate problem.

If the field is the time string itself — if the particle's local
geometry extends outward and curves space around it — then
disturbances in that geometry should propagate. And if they propagate
faster than the particle that creates them, you have a causality
violation. An effect preceding its cause. A signal arriving before
it was sent.

Physics has a strict rule about this. Nothing travels faster than
light. Not because of an arbitrary convention but because faster-
than-light signaling between events would mean that in some reference
frame, the signal arrives before it departs. Cause and effect would
reverse. The universe's logical structure would unravel.

So the field was kept local. Internal. The parabola was the particle's
own geometry, not something that propagated outward into global space.

But then a new question appeared, harder than the first: if the field
is purely local and internal, how does it reach anything? Forces
affect distant objects. Gravity bends the path of a planet. The
electromagnetic field of an electron affects another electron meters
away. If the geometry is locked inside the particle, how does it
produce global effects?

The answer required understanding what happens when the local
geometry spreads.

## The Flux Tube: FTL Within a Single Frame

A particle moving through the tensioned time substrate does not just
carry its local geometry with it. Under the right conditions — when
the curvature gradient is steep enough, when the winding structure
encounters a barrier — the particle's 1D geometry spreads across
a single quantum of time. A single frame.

This spreading is faster than light. Not approximately faster —
instantaneously faster. A single frame, multiple positions: the
velocity implied is infinite. The particle occupies an extended
region of space within a time interval so short that no light-speed
signal could cross that region.

The result is a flux tube: the particle's 1D geometry, extended
across a spatial region, carrying the full winding structure along
its length but projected — flattened — from the rich internal
geometry of the particle down to a 1D thread through 3D space.

This is not a violation of the causality rule in the sense that
creates paradoxes. The spreading is within the particle's local
frame. Nothing in global spacetime moved from point A to point B
faster than light. The particle's internal geometry spread, and
what we observe in global spacetime is the projection of that
spreading. Global causality — the prohibition on closed timelike
curves, on effects preceding causes in every reference frame — is
preserved because the flux tube is a constraint on the particle's
geometry, not a signal traveling between two events in spacetime.

But the spreading itself is genuinely FTL. This is not a
mathematical artifact. We observe it.

## Tunneling: The Direct Observation

Quantum tunneling — the ability of a particle to pass through a
barrier it classically cannot cross — has puzzled physicists since
the 1920s. A particle without enough energy to climb over a wall
nevertheless appears on the other side. The wavefunction extends
through the barrier and produces a transmitted amplitude. This much
has been understood and used in technology for decades:
tunnel diodes, scanning tunneling microscopes, nuclear fusion in
stars, radioactive decay.

What has not been understood is how long it takes.

In 1962, Thomas Hartman calculated the tunneling time — how long
a particle spends traversing the barrier — and found something
disturbing: for sufficiently thick barriers, the tunneling time
becomes independent of barrier width. The particle traverses a
thicker barrier in the same time as a thinner one. For thick
enough barriers, the implied velocity is superluminal. This is
the Hartman effect.

The subsequent six decades of experimental measurement have
confirmed it repeatedly. Single photons tunneling through photonic
barriers in the early 1990s were measured at superluminal group
velocities. Microwave experiments by Enders and Nimtz in 1992
measured tunneling signals traveling at 4.7 times the speed of
light. Phonons, photons, and electrons all display the same
universal behavior at the barrier entrance.

As the Quanta Magazine summary stated in 2021: quantum tunneling
has proven to be "incurably, robustly superluminal" across six
decades of careful redefinition and remeasurement. Nimtz and
colleagues summarized their findings directly: experiments with
evanescent modes and tunneling particles have shown that their
signal velocity may be faster than light, they are nonlocal and
act at a distance, and their properties are not compatible with
special relativity as conventionally applied.

The standard defense — that the group delay is the lifetime of
stored energy escaping through both ends of the barrier rather
than a transit time — resolves the mathematical paradox but not
the physical one. Stored energy still has to escape. It still
escapes faster than light allows. The geometric explanation is
more satisfying: the particle's 1D geometry spreads across the
frame as a flux tube. The barrier is a 3D object in global
spacetime. A 1D projection spreading FTL within a local frame
does not encounter a 3D barrier the same way a classical particle
does. It projects through.

## The Information Claim

The standard response to all FTL observations in quantum mechanics
is a single sentence: "You cannot use this to send information
faster than light."

This response is correct in a narrow technical sense and wrong
in every practical sense. Here is why.

**Bell inequality violations.**

In 1964, John Bell proved that if nature is locally realistic —
if distant particles have definite properties before measurement
and can only influence each other through signals traveling at
or below light speed — then correlations between measurements
on entangled particles must satisfy a specific mathematical
inequality.

Every experiment ever performed has violated it.

Aspect, Dalibard, and Roger in 1982 measured entangled photon
pairs with time-varying analyzers switching faster than the
photon transit time between detectors — closing the locality
loophole — and found violations of Bell's inequality by 5
standard deviations, in perfect agreement with quantum mechanics.

Weihs, Jennewein, Simon, Weinfurter, and Zeilinger in 1998
enforced strict Einstein locality conditions — random analyzer
settings, physically separated stations, independent data
registration — and found violations by over 30 standard deviations.

In 2015, Hensen and colleagues at Delft achieved the first
loophole-free Bell test: both the detection loophole and the
locality loophole closed simultaneously. Entangled electron
spins in diamonds, separated by 1.3 kilometers — far enough
that no light-speed signal could cross the distance during the
measurement. Violation of the Bell inequality confirmed.
The experiment was repeated with the same setup, giving
S = 2.38 ± 0.14, well above the local-realist limit of 2.
In the same year, Giustina et al. and Shalm et al. independently
confirmed loophole-free violations with entangled photons.

The 2022 Nobel Prize in Physics was awarded to Clauser, Aspect,
and Zeilinger for this body of work. The Nobel citation explicitly
states that the experiments confirmed the violation of Bell
inequalities and established that nature is nonlocal.

The correlations between entangled particles are instantaneous
regardless of separation. When particle A is measured, particle B
— wherever it is — instantly acquires a definite correlated state.
That state carries information about the measurement of A: which
basis was used, what outcome was obtained. That information exists
at particle B before any light-speed signal from A could have
arrived.

The defense — "you can't use this to send a message" — depends on
the fact that the outcome of measuring A is random. You can't
choose what result you get at A, so you can't encode a chosen
message in the correlation. This is true. But the correlation
itself is information. The spin of B is determined by the
measurement of A, instantaneously, across any distance.
That determination is a physical fact that came into existence
FTL.

**Quantum teleportation.**

In 1993, Bennett, Brassard, Crépeau, Jozsa, Peres, and Wootters
proposed quantum teleportation: the complete transfer of an unknown
quantum state from one particle to another, using an entangled
pair as the channel. Experimentally demonstrated in 1997 by
Bouwmeester, Pan, Mattle, Eibl, Weinfurter, and Zeilinger using
entangled photons. The polarization state of an initial photon
was transferred to a second photon arbitrarily far away.

The quantum state — which is the complete information content of
the particle — moved instantaneously through the entangled channel.
The standard defense: "a classical channel is also required to
complete the teleportation." This is true. Bob needs to receive
a 2-bit classical message from Alice to know which unitary
operation to apply to his particle. But the quantum state itself —
the information — moved FTL through the entangled channel.
The classical message tells Bob how to read it, not what it is.

Quantum teleportation has since been demonstrated with photons,
atoms, electrons, and superconducting circuits. In 2017,
Ren, Xu, Yong, and Pan's team demonstrated satellite-based
quantum teleportation to the Micius satellite at distances
exceeding 1,400 kilometers. The quantum state transferred
instantaneously. The classical confirmation signal traveled
at light speed.

Quantum computing uses entanglement as its primary computational
resource. The correlations between qubits — instantaneous,
independent of separation — are what give quantum computers
their advantage over classical ones. Without FTL quantum
correlations, quantum computing does not exist. We have built
machines that run on FTL information transfer. Those machines work.

**Cooper pairs.**

Superconductivity — zero electrical resistance below a critical
temperature — is explained by the BCS theory of Bardeen, Cooper,
and Schrieffer (Nobel Prize, 1972). Its central object is the
Cooper pair: two electrons bound together across the Fermi surface,
correlated regardless of their separation within the material.

As Britannica's description of BCS theory states: Cooper discovered
that the motions of all Cooper pairs within a single superconductor
are correlated — they constitute a system that functions as a
single entity. Paired electrons may be hundreds of nanometers apart
— three orders of magnitude larger than the lattice spacing —
yet their states are instantaneously correlated. The pair condensate
is a macroscopic quantum state where FTL correlation is not a
curiosity but the operating mechanism.

Without the instantaneous correlation there is no Cooper pair.
Without Cooper pairs there is no superconducting condensate.
Without the condensate there is no zero resistance, no Meissner
effect, no flux quantization. Superconductivity is FTL correlation
deployed at macroscopic scale. It runs the MRI machine in every
hospital. It runs the particle detectors at the Large Hadron Collider.
It runs the superconducting qubits in quantum computers.

We have built civilization-scale technology on FTL quantum
correlations. The claim that these correlations cannot carry
information is a definition — a very specific, very narrow
definition of "information" that excludes correlated quantum
states. In any operational sense of the word, information about
particle A is present at particle B, instantaneously, across
any distance, in all three of these systems.

**The Josephson Effect — FTL at the Voltage Standard.**

In 1962, Brian Josephson — a 23-year-old graduate student at
Cambridge — predicted that Cooper pairs could tunnel coherently
through a thin insulating barrier between two superconductors,
producing a supercurrent with no applied voltage. Not a trickle.
A sustained, continuous current flowing through an insulator
with nothing driving it, because the quantum phase coherence
of the Cooper pairs on both sides of the barrier is maintained
instantaneously across the gap.

Anderson and Rowell at Bell Laboratories confirmed it
experimentally in 1963. Josephson received the Nobel Prize in
Physics in 1973.

The Josephson junction is now the operating principle of
superconducting qubits — the building blocks of quantum computers
from IBM, Google, and every major quantum computing program in
the world. It is the basis of SQUID magnetometers used in brain
imaging and geological surveying. And it is the international
voltage standard: the AC Josephson effect produces voltage steps
so precisely quantized that it has replaced all previous voltage
references worldwide. Every national metrology institute on earth
uses it.

We did not just observe FTL quantum correlation in a physics
experiment. We built the international voltage standard on it.

## The Geometric Resolution

The GBP framework does not resolve the tension between FTL
correlation and causality by denying either. Both are real.

FTL correlation is real. It is observed in tunneling, in Bell
violations, in quantum teleportation, in Cooper pairs. The
correlations are instantaneous. The information is present before
any light-speed signal could carry it.

Global causality is also real. No experiment has ever produced
a closed timelike curve. No effect has ever been observed to
precede its cause in every reference frame simultaneously.
The universe's logical structure remains intact.

The resolution is geometric. Two entangled particles share a
topological connection — they are part of the same winding
structure, the same flux tube extended across space. The flux
tube is the particle's 1D local geometry spread across a frame.
It does not travel through global spacetime. It exists as a
constraint on the shared geometry of the entangled pair.

When one particle is measured, the measurement is a local
geometric event: the winding collapses to a definite lane.
That collapse propagates through the shared geometric structure
instantaneously — not through global spacetime, but through
the topology that connects the pair. Global spacetime sees
the result of the collapse, not the collapse itself traveling
as a signal.

This is why FTL correlation cannot be used to send a chosen
message. The choice of measurement basis at one end does
change the probability distribution at the other end, but the
outcome at any one end is random and uncontrollable. The shared
geometry is a correlation structure, not a transmission channel.
Information about the correlation is instantaneously present
everywhere the topology extends. The ability to encode a
chosen message requires controlling the random outcome, which
requires breaking the correlation — which destroys the topology
that made the FTL connection possible in the first place.

FTL without signaling is not a paradox that requires a
definitional escape. It is the natural consequence of a
universe where geometry is local to particles, particles can
share geometric connections across space, and those connections
propagate within the shared topology rather than through the
global spacetime background.

The flux tube is FTL. That is not a problem.
That is what flux tubes are.
