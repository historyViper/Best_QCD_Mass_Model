## 1. The Standard Picture and Its Hidden Assumption

Quantum field theory separates matter and force: fermion fields (quarks) couple to gauge boson fields (gluons) through an interaction Lagrangian. The proton is a bound state — three valence quarks in a color-singlet configuration, held together by a sea of virtual gluons that carry the strong force between them.

This picture is extraordinarily successful computationally. Lattice QCD reproduces hadron masses to sub-percent precision by solving the coupled quark-gluon dynamics numerically. QED predicts the electron magnetic moment to 12 decimal places. The framework works.

But it contains a hidden assumption: that the separation between matter field and gauge field is ontologically real — that quarks and gluons are genuinely different kinds of things, and the proton is genuinely a composite object assembled from components.

This assumption is never tested directly. We observe jets, cross-sections, and decay products. We infer quarks and gluons from those observations using the field theory framework. The inference is valid within that framework. But the question of whether the quark-gluon decomposition reflects the actual geometry of nature, or whether it is a useful but ultimately artificial decomposition of a single geometric object, remains open.

This paper argues for the latter.

## 3. The Quark-Gluon Duality **(H)**

The most important consequence of the particle-field identity is the quark-gluon relationship.

### 3.1 The Standard Picture

In QCD, a quark carries color charge (red, green, or blue). Gluons carry color-anticolor pairs and mediate color exchange between quarks. When a quark emits a gluon, it changes color: a red quark emits a red-antiblue gluon and becomes blue. Quarks and gluons are different particles with different quantum numbers.

In the GBP framework, a quark is a T1 Möbius winding viewed from the **boundary** of the toroid — the observable face that projects onto spacetime. The gluon is the **same T1 winding** viewed from the **interior** — the Hamiltonian path that the winding takes through the interior of the Y-junction geometry.

These are not two different objects. They are one object with an inside and an outside.

The inside (Hamiltonian interior, "hyperspace" side) is the gluon.  
The outside (boundary projection, observable side) is the quark.

**Why this works geometrically:**

The T1 Möbius winding has two natural perspectives:

1. **Boundary perspective:** Stand outside the toroid and observe the winding cross the boundary. You see a winding number, a projection weight P(r) = sin²(rπ/15), and a color label corresponding to which lane r ∈ Z₃₀* is active. This is the quark — a projected color charge on the spacetime boundary.

2. **Interior perspective:** Stand inside the toroid and observe the Hamiltonian path that the winding takes through the interior. You see a path through the Y-junction geometry that carries a color-anticolor pair (because the Hamiltonian path must enter and exit the junction, creating a directed flow with both a source color and a destination color). This is the gluon — the interior color-flow of the same winding.

The color change when a quark emits a gluon is not the quark and gluon doing something to each other. It is the single T1 winding reshaping its boundary projection — changing which face of the toroid is the "outside." The gluon is not emitted from the quark; the gluon IS the transition between two boundary states of the same toroid.

This picture makes the pion relationship natural. A pion (quark-antiquark pair) is a T1 + anti-T1 system — one winding and its mirror. When a proton and neutron exchange a pion during nuclear interaction, what happens is:

This is why the pion takes the form of the quark: **the pion IS a quark pair in transit**. It has the same toroid structure as the quarks it comes from, because it is the same toroid structure — temporarily detached and moving.
